<section>
  <div class="container container-narrow">
    <p class="section-eyebrow">Mission</p>
    <h2 class="section-title">Sovereign AI primitives</h2>
    <p>
      LegionForge is built around one bet: <strong>the future of consequential AI is
      local-first and security-native</strong>. Cloud agents are powerful but unauditable;
      open-source frameworks are auditable but unguarded. The interesting space is the
      intersection — software that gives you AI capabilities <em>without</em> giving up
      your sovereignty over the data, the keys, or the threat model.
    </p>
    <p>
      Every project under the LegionForge GitHub org is open-source, runs on your hardware,
      and treats security as a foundation rather than a feature.
    </p>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <div class="container">
    <p class="section-eyebrow">License model</p>
    <h2 class="section-title">Open-source with a sustainable funding path</h2>
    <p>
      The main framework is <strong>AGPL-3.0</strong> with a <strong>commercial license
      option</strong>. Personal use, open-source projects, and internal company use stay
      under AGPL for free. Companies that want to embed LegionForge in a proprietary
      product or run it as a paid network service without source disclosure can purchase
      a commercial license.
    </p>
    <p>
      <a href="/guardian/">Guardian</a> is <strong>MIT-licensed</strong> independently —
      the deliberate choice so it can drop into any agent framework regardless of that
      framework's license.
    </p>
    <p>
      Most tools (dev-rig, mcp-probe, hermes-tool-test-suite, etc.) are MIT. Apps like
      Jeli and ADHD-OS follow the framework's AGPL.
    </p>
    <p>
      Commercial license inquiries: <a href="mailto:jp@legionforge.org">jp@legionforge.org</a>
    </p>
  </div>
</section>
